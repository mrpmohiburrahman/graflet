## Why software makers are leaving Gumroad

Gumroad works well for selling a $10 PDF. But for software businesses building a scalable online business with recurring income at its core, its limits show quickly.

What starts as a simple setup eventually becomes a set of workarounds that don’t scale well for growing businesses.

### Fees that add up quickly

Gumroad charges **10% + $0.50 per transaction**, plus standard payment processing fees, bringing effective costs to roughly 13%–16% on most direct sales.

Sales through Gumroad Discover (its built-in marketplace) take a flat 30% cut.

For a software maker doing $5,000/month, that’s roughly $650–$800 lost in fees alone, which becomes even more significant as you scale and look for platforms with lower transaction fees or more predictable pricing structures.

## What to look for in a Gumroad alternative

The right Gumroad alternative depends on what you sell (SaaS, desktop apps, AI-built apps, WordPress plugins and themes) and how you sell it (subscriptions, one-time payments, freemium).

But for software businesses, only a few criteria actually matter.

### Fees and total effective cost

Don’t look at headline pricing alone. Compare the full effective fee: platform percentage, payment gateway fees, fixed per-transaction charges, and add-ons like affiliates or email features.

A “5% platform fee” can quickly become much higher once you include PayPal surcharges, FX fees, and extra tooling.

Gumroad typically lands around **13%–16% effective fees on direct sales**, while its marketplace sales take a flat **30% cut**.

### Tax and compliance ownership

This is one of the biggest structural differences between platforms.

[Merchant of record](merchant-of-record.md) platforms like **Freemius, Paddle, Lemon Squeezy, and FastSpring** act as the legal seller. They handle VAT, sales tax calculation, filing, and remittance globally.

Gateway-based tools like **Easy Digital Downloads, WooCommerce, and SendOwl** may cost less upfront, but they leave tax registration, compliance, and audit responsibility on you.

If you sell internationally, this gap compounds quickly as revenue grows.

### Software-specific features

Software has different billing needs than digital downloads. Look for support for:

- License keys and activation logic
- Subscription lifecycles (trials, upgrades, downgrades, proration)
- Per-seat or usage-based billing
- Dunning and failed-payment recovery

**The key question:** Does the platform match how your product actually evolves over time?

### Checkout and conversion tooling

Checkout influences how many visitors become paying customers through pricing presentation, UX friction, and payment options.

Evaluate:

- Hosted vs. embedded checkout
- Mobile experience and localization
- Local payment methods

Then look at conversion tools:

- [Coupons and discounts](help-documentation-marketing-automation-special-coupons-discounts.md)
- Upsells and order bumps
- [Cart abandonment flows](help-documentation-marketing-automation-cart-abandonment-recovery.md)
- Exit-intent offers

Stronger conversion tooling can directly impact revenue by reducing friction in checkout flows and leading to fewer abandoned carts.

### Customer ownership and data control

Check how much control you actually have. Can you:

- Export customers easily
- Move data without friction
- Use your own domain and branding
- Own your email list

Strong portability reduces platform risk, especially when pricing or policies change over time.

### Ecosystem and integrations

No platform operates in isolation. Look for integrations with:

- Email tools
- Analytics platforms
- CRMs
- License servers
- Automation tools like Zapier or Make

A strong API and webhook system becomes critical once you start automating provisioning, refunds, and entitlement logic.

### Reliability, support, and roadmap

Beyond features, evaluate stability. Consider:

- Uptime and payment reliability
- Backing and payment providers
- Product roadmap velocity
- Support quality and response times

Also pay attention to positioning: platforms built for enterprise SaaS often feel over-engineered for indie developers, while indie-focused tools may lack depth at scale.
